## 1. Introduction

Rice, known locally as *Dhan*, is the most crucial staple crop in Nepal. It holds immense economic, social, and nutritional value, acting as the backbone of the country's agricultural economy and food security. Cultivating rice successfully ensures both food for the family and income for rural communities.

### Role in Nepalese Agriculture and Economy

Rice contributes significantly to the agricultural Gross Domestic Product (AGDP) of Nepal. It is grown across diverse agro-ecological zones, from the low-lying plains of the Terai to the high-altitude hills. The Terai region is considered the "granary" of Nepal, producing the largest share of the nation's rice, though mid-hill and high-hill river vallear-round irrigation, farmers also cultivate Spring Rice (*Chaite Dhan*), which is planted in February–March and harvested in June–July.

Cultivation spans three major regions:

1. **Terai and Inner Terai (Lowlands):** Characterized by high temperatures and fertile alluvial soils.
2. **Mid-Hills:** Grown in terraced fields (*Khet*) where water can be retained.
3. **High-Hills:** Grown up to altitudes of around 2,600 meters using cold-tolerant varieties.

## 2. Planning Before Cultivation

Successful rice production requires careful planning, proper selection of seed varieties, and thorough land preparation before the first seed touches the soil.

### Crop Varieties

Selecting the right rice variety is the foundation of a good harvest. In Nepal, varieties are categorized based on their adaptability to specific altitudes, ecological zones, and water availability.

| Variety Type | Description & Characteristics | Examples in Nepal |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Local Varieties** | Low-yielding but resilient to local stresses and pests; often preferred for specific tastes or cultural ceremonies. | *Anadi*, *Tauli*, *Jethobudo* (Pokhara) |
| **Improved Varieties** | Open-pollinated varieties bred for higher yields, disease resistance, and responsiveness to fertilizers. | *Radha-4*, *Sabitri*, *Ram*, *Hardinath-1*, *Khumal-4*, *Sukhadhan* series |
| **Hybrid Varieties** | First-generation crosses that offer significantly higher yield potential but require buying new seeds every season. | *Dyanga*, *US-312* |

#### Selection Criteria

When choosing a variety, consider the following factors:

* **Regional Suitability:** Lowland Terai varieties like *Sabitri* or *Radha-4* will not mature properly or survive the cold temperatures of the High-Hills. For hills, choose varieties like *Khumal-4* or *Khumal-11*.
* **Water Availability:** If your fields are rain-fed and prone to drought, select drought-tolerant varieties like *Sukhadhan-3* or *Sukhadhan-5*. For flood-prone areas, use flood-tolerant varieties like *Swarna Sub-1*.
* **Maturity Period:** Early maturing varieties (110–120 days) are ideal if you plan to plant a winter crop like wheat or potato immediately after the rice harvest.

### Climate and Soil Requirements

Rice thrives in hot and humid climates. The ideal temperature range for rice growth is **21°C to 37°C**. Low temperatures during the flowering stage can cause poor grain filling and significantly reduce yields. The crop requires abundant sunshine and a reliable water supply, either through timely monsoon rainfall or irrigation.

#### Soil and Land Suitability

Rice can grow in various soil types, but **clayey loam to heavy clay soils** are ideal. These soils have excellent water-holding capacity, preventing water from draining away too quickly.

* **Soil pH:** Rice grows best in slightly acidic to neutral soils with a pH between **5.5 and 7.0**.
* **Organic Matter:** Soil rich in organic matter retains nutrients longer, reducing fertilizer wastage.
* **Drainage:** While rice needs standing water, the field must have proper bunds and drainage channels to control water levels during heavy downpours.

### Land Preparation

Thorough land preparation creates an ideal environment for root establishment and destroys weeds and pests.

1. **Clearing:** Remove stubble, weeds, and debris from the previous crop. Repair and compact the field bunds (*Kanla* or *Baan*) to prevent water leakage.
2. **Primary Ploughing:** Plough the dry field 2–3 weeks before sowing or transplanting using a tractor, power tiller, or traditional bullock-drawn plough to loosen the soil.
3. **Compost Incorporation:** Apply well-decomposed Farmyard Manure (FYM) or compost uniformly across the field during the primary ploughing so it mixes deep into the soil.
4. **Puddling:** Flood the field with 5–10 cm of water and plough it again. Puddling breaks down soil clods, churns the soil into a soft mud paste, and creates an impermeable layer beneath the surface. This layer prevents water from seeping away into the deep ground.
5. **Levelling:** Use a wooden levelling board (*Pata*) to make the field perfectly flat. Proper levelling ensures uniform water depth across the entire plot, preventing dry patches or waterlogging spots.

### Seed Selection and Treatment

Healthy plants come from high-quality seeds. Always buy **certified or truthful-labelled seeds** from trusted cooperatives or agro-vets. If using your own seed from the previous harvest, ensure they are clean, plump, free from weed seeds, and have a germination rate above 80%.

#### Seed Floating Test

To separate healthy seeds from empty or damaged ones:

1. Dissolve 100 grams of common salt in 1 liter of clean water.
2. Pour the seeds into the saltwater solution.
3. Stir well. Healthy, heavy seeds will sink to the bottom, while lightweight, diseased, or empty seeds will float.
4. Skim off and discard the floating seeds. Remove the sunken seeds, wash them thoroughly with fresh water to remove salt, and use them for sowing.

#### Seed Treatment

Treating seeds protects the young seedlings from soil-borne and seed-borne diseases.

* **Fungicide Treatment:** Mix 2 grams of Carbendazim or Mancozeb per 1 kg of seed. Mix thoroughly in dry conditions or after soaking the seeds to protect against blast and foot rot.
* **Biological Treatment:** Alternatively, treat seeds with *Trichoderma viride* at 5–10 grams per kg of seed for organic or low-chemical farming.

### Planting Methods and Nursery Management

In Nepal, rice is planted either by transplanting seedlings from a nursery or by Direct Seeded Rice (DSR) methods.

#### Nursery Preparation (Wet-Bed Method)

* **Seed Rate:** 40 to 50 kg of seed is sufficient to plant one hectare of land (approx. 2 to 2.5 kg per ropani).
* **Nursery Bed Layout:** Prepare raised beds 1 meter wide and of a convenient length, leaving 30 cm walking channels between beds for drainage and weeding.
* **Sowing:** Sow pre-germinated seeds uniformly on the muddy bed. Keep the bed moist but not submerged for the first few days. Gradually increase the water level as seedlings grow.

#### Transplanting

Seedlings are ready for transplanting when they develop 4–5 leaves, which usually takes **21 to 25 days** for normal varieties, and up to 30 days in cooler hill regions.

* **Spacing:** Plant seedlings at a spacing of **20 cm between rows and 15 cm between hills**.
* **Depth:** Plant 2–3 seedlings per hill at a shallow depth of **2 to 3 cm**. Planting too deep delays tillering (the growth of side shoots).

## 3. Growing and Managing the Crop

Careful management during the growth phases maximizes tillering and grain development.

### Crop Growth Stages

Understanding growth stages helps in timing irrigation and fertilizer applications:

1. **Nursery & Establishment Stage (0–30 days):** Seedling growth and initial root establishment after transplanting.
2. **Vegetative/Tillering Stage (30–60 days):** The plant produces multiple tillers (stems). Maximum tillering determines the final number of grain pans.
3. **Panicle Initiation & Flowering Stage (60–90 days):** The plant stops growing taller and begins developing the grain heads. Flowers open and self-pollinate.
4. **Grain Filling & Maturity Stage (90–130 days):** Milky fluid fills the grains, hardens into rice, and turns golden-yellow.

### Water and Irrigation Management

Rice is a water-loving crop, but it does not need deep water continuously. Managing water carefully saves labor and prevents disease.

* **Critical Stages:** Water stress must be avoided during three critical periods:
1. Immediately after transplanting (for root establishment).
2. Active tillering stage.
3. Flowering and panicle initiation.


* **Water Depth:** Maintain a shallow water level of **3 to 5 cm** during tillering and flowering.
* **Drainage:** Drain the field completely **10 to 15 days before harvesting**. This accelerates uniform ripening of grains and hardens the soil, making harvesting easier.

### Nutrient Management

Rice requires a balanced application of primary macronutrients: Nitrogen (N) for green growth, Phosphorus (P) for root growth, and Potassium (K) for disease resistance and grain quality.

#### Recommended Fertilizer Dosage (per Hectare)

For improved lowland varieties, the standard recommendation is **100 kg Nitrogen, 60 kg Phosphorus, and 40 kg Potassium (100:60:40 N:P:K kg/ha)**.

* **Basal Dose:** Apply the full dose of Phosphorus, full dose of Potassium, and half of the Nitrogen during final land preparation (puddling).
* **First Top Dressing:** Apply one-quarter of the Nitrogen at the active tillering stage (approx. 25–30 days after transplanting), immediately after the first weeding.
* **Second Top Dressing:** Apply the remaining one-quarter of Nitrogen at the panicle initiation stage (approx. 50–60 days after transplanting).

> **Farmer Tip:** Always apply top-dressed nitrogen when the field has moist soil but no standing water, or immediately weed the field so the fertilizer mixes into the mud rather than floating away in standing water.

### Weed Management

Weeds compete fiercely with rice for sunlight, water, and nutrients, reducing yields by up to 30% if left unchecked.

* **Manual and Mechanical Control:** Perform the first weeding 25–30 days after transplanting using a hand hoe or a mechanical cono-weeder. A second weeding should be done 50–60 days after transplanting. Cono-weeders bury the weeds into the mud, converting them into green manure.
* **Chemical Control:** Where labor is scarce, apply pre-emergence herbicides like Pretilachlor or Butachlor within 3–5 days of transplanting when the weeds have not yet emerged. Ensure a thin layer of standing water is maintained in the field for the herbicide to work effectively.

## 4. Keeping the Crop Healthy

Protecting the crop from diseases, insect pests, and nutrient deficiencies requires regular field monitoring and quick action.

### Major Diseases

* **Rice Blast (Fungal):**
* *Symptoms:* Eye-shaped or diamond-shaped spots with grey centers and brown borders appear on the leaves. If it attacks the neck of the grain head (Neck Blast), the panicle rots and breaks, leaving empty, white grains.
* *Management:* Avoid excessive nitrogen fertilizer. Spray Carbendazim (1 gm/liter of water) or Tricyclazole (0.6 gm/liter of water) upon noticing early spots.


* **Bacterial Leaf Blight (BLB):**
* *Symptoms:* Wavy, yellowish-to-grey stripes appear starting from the tips of the leaves and moving downward along the margins.
* *Management:* Maintain proper drainage. Avoid clipping seedling tips during transplanting. Spray a solution of copper oxychloride mixed with a bactericide if infections are severe.



### Major Insect Pests

* **Stem Borer:**
* *Symptoms:* The larvae tunnel into the stem. Attacks during the vegetative stage cause the central leaf shoot to dry up and die, known as **"Dead Heart"**. Attacks during the flowering stage result in empty, upright, white panicles known as **"White Head"**.
* *Management:* Use light traps to catch adult moths. Cut and destroy affected plants. Apply granular Cartap Hydrochloride or Carbofuran in the soil if damage exceeds economic thresholds.


* **Brown Plant Hopper (BPH):**
* *Symptoms:* These tiny insects suck sap from the base of the rice stems. In large numbers, they cause patches of the field to dry out rapidly and turn brown, an effect known as **"Hopper Burn"**.
* *Management:* Avoid dense planting; leave 30 cm wide alleyways every 2 meters to let light and air inside the crop canopy. Spray Imidacloprid or Buprofezin directly at the base of the plants.



### Integrated Pest Management (IPM) Practices

Relying entirely on chemical sprays harms the environment and eliminates beneficial insects like spiders and ladybird beetles that naturally eat pests. Follow these IPM steps:

1. **Field Sanitation:** Keep field bunds clean to remove alternative homes for pests.
2. **Crop Rotation:** Rotate rice with non-cereal crops like lentils or mustard during the winter to break pest life cycles.
3. **Regular Monitoring:** Walk through your field twice a week. Look closely at the base of the plants for hoppers and check leaves for spots.

## 5. Sustainable Farming and Post-Harvest Management

Sustainable practices keep the soil fertile for future generations, while proper post-harvest handling ensures that none of your hard work goes to waste.

### Sustainable and Organic Soil Practices

Continuous chemical fertilizer use without organic matter degrades soil structure over time.

* **Green Manuring:** Plant *Dhaincha* (*Sesbania*) or sunn hemp in the field during April–May and plough it back into the soil while it is green, 2 weeks before rice transplanting. This fixes natural nitrogen and adds abundant organic matter.
* **Straw Biochar and Compost:** Return decomposed rice straw or farmyard compost to the fields to maintain organic carbon levels.

### Climate Adaptation

Climate change causes unpredictable monsoons, leading to droughts or sudden floods in Nepal.

* **Drought Management:** Use Direct Seeded Rice (DSR) or Alternate Wetting and Drying (AWD) irrigation methods to save water. Plant drought-hardy varieties like *Sukhadhan-6*.
* **Flood Management:** In lowlands prone to short-term submergence, use *Swarna Sub-1*, which can survive completely underwater for up to two weeks.

### Harvesting

Harvesting at the correct time prevents grains from cracking, shattering, or being eaten by birds and rodents.

```
Maturity Indicator: Grains are ready for harvest when more than 80–85% of the panicles on the plant have turned from green to a golden-yellow color, and the moisture level in the grain drops to around 20–22%.

```

* **Method:** Cut the crop manually using sickles close to the ground. Leave the harvested crop in neat rows in the field for 2–3 days to dry slightly under the sun.
* **Threshing:** Thresh the dried stalks manually by beating them against a wooden log or drum, or use a mechanical pedal thresher or tractor-driven thresher to separate the grains from the straw.

### Post-Harvest Handling and Storage

Improper storage leads to mold growth, grain rot, and heavy losses from weevils and rats.

1. **Winnowing and Cleaning:** Use a traditional winnowing fan (*Nanglo*) or mechanical winnowers to blow away dust, chaff, empty grains, and weed seeds from the threshed paddy.
2. **Grading:** Separate broken grains from whole grains to secure better prices in the market.
3. **Drying:** This is the most crucial step. Spread the grains on clean plastic sheets or clean concrete floors under the sun. **Dry the paddy until the moisture content drops to 12–14%.** You can test this by biting a grain: if it cracks with a sharp, crisp sound, it is dry enough for storage.
4. **Storage Structures:** Store the dried grains in airtight containers to prevent moisture absorption and pest attacks. Excellent traditional and modern options include:
* **SuperGrainbags:** Heavy-duty plastic liner bags inside regular jute sacks that cut off oxygen, suffocating insects and preventing mold.
* **Metal Bins:** Airtight galvanized iron drums that protect grains effectively from rodents.
* **Bhakari or Kothi:** Traditional mud-plastered bamboo structures, provided they are sealed properly against damp ground.



### Good Agricultural Practices (GAP) Summary

To maintain food safety, high market quality, and farmer health, always keep written records of your expenses, fertilizer quantities, and planting dates. Never apply chemical pesticides within 20 days of harvest, and always wear protective clothing, gloves, and masks when handling or spraying chemicals. Dispose of empty chemical bottles safely by puncturing them and burying them away from water sources.
